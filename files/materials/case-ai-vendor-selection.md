# Selecting an AI Platform Partner

**Associated course:** AI for Business  
**Format:** Decision case with exhibits  
**Suggested duration:** 90 minutes

## Abstract

A business unit compares competing AI proposals with different value claims, data requirements and implementation risks.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this case discussion, participants should be able to:

- Compare vendor value claims against the evidence actually supplied to support them.
- Identify the data access, integration and governance obligations hidden inside a platform proposal.
- Distinguish pilot performance from expected performance at operating scale.
- Construct a defensible recommendation under incomplete information and commercial pressure.

## Case context

A business unit within a mid-sized services organisation has been asked to select an AI platform partner for customer-facing automation. Three vendors have responded. Each proposal quantifies benefit differently: one leads with accuracy improvements measured on its own benchmark, one leads with headcount savings modelled over three years, and one declines to forecast benefit at all and instead proposes a paid discovery phase.

The proposals also differ in what they require from the organisation. Two require broad access to historical customer records; the third operates on a narrower feature set but needs a data pipeline the organisation does not currently have. Procurement has scored the proposals on price and contractual terms. The business unit must now form a technical and managerial view.

## The decision

Participants take the position of the business unit lead. They must recommend one proposal, reject the others with stated reasons, and specify the conditions under which they would revisit the decision. The recommendation goes to a steering group that has already been told the programme will deliver benefit in the current financial year.

## Discussion questions

1. What is each vendor actually claiming, and what evidence would be needed to accept the claim?
2. Which proposal carries the greatest risk of performance degrading between pilot and full operation, and why?
3. What does each proposal require the organisation to give up - in data access, in flexibility, in future switching cost?
4. How should the business unit lead handle a benefit expectation that has already been communicated upward?
5. What would you write into the contract that none of the three vendors has offered?

## Suggested teaching sequence

Timings assume a 90-minute session and should be adjusted to the cohort.

| Stage | Minutes | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Framing | 9 | Establish the decision and the constraints on it |
| Small groups | 27 | Groups work the exhibits and form a position |
| Plenary | 31 | Positions are stated and tested against the evidence |
| Complication | 14 | Introduce the withheld detail and re-test the positions |
| Close | 9 | Generalise to the participants' own contexts |
